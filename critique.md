Because winning is a **relative ranking problem**, not a “good project / bad project” problem.

Your probability is low mainly for 6 hard reasons:

1. **Base rate is brutal**
- With ~4,000+ participants, even strong projects start with very low odds.

2. **You’re competing against submission quality, not just code**
- Winners usually have elite demo storytelling + crisp Devpost packaging.
- Your repo still looked more “engineering work-in-progress” than “submission-optimized package.”

3. **Demo score risk (30%) is high**
- Judges may not run your app.
- If video/diagram/cloud-proof aren’t top-tier, you lose a huge chunk regardless of backend quality.

4. **Reliability proof is not yet strong enough (Technical 30%)**
- No formal test suite and limited hard evidence of robustness under live failure modes (timeouts, reconnects, noisy audio, edge cases).

5. **Differentiation risk in a crowded category**
- “Vision + voice + agent + work-order” is strong, but others will have similar stacks.
- Winners usually show one unforgettable differentiator and quantify impact.

6. **Execution consistency across all artifacts**
- You have good core architecture, but hackathons reward teams that are equally strong in product polish, narrative, and judging materials.

Short version: your project is good, but **good is not enough** in a high-competition hackathon. You need “top 1–3% submission polish + reliability proof + demo punch” to move into likely-win territory.
